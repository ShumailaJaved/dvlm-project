\begin{abstract}
A persistent problem in multimodal language models is that the connector between the vision encoder and the language model processes all image patches the same way, regardless of what the question is asking. This means the model has to figure out visual relevance on its own, a task it often fails at. To tackle this, we build on LLaVA-1.5-7B~\citep{liu2024llava} and propose the \emph{Mixture of Connectors} (MoC), which routes each input to one of four structurally different connector experts depending on the question. Our core contribution is the \emph{Question-Conditioned Gating Projector} (QCGP, $E_4$), which computes a per-patch gate based on how relevant each image region is to the current question. A small question-conditioned router selects one expert per sample and is trained with a load-balancing loss to keep all experts in use. We evaluate on ScienceQA~\citep{lu2022scienceqa} and find that $E_2$, $E_3$, and $E_4$ all trained from random initialization , reach only 34--39\% image accuracy compared to $E_1$'s 65.6\%, which reuses LLaVA's pretrained projector. This tells us that having good initial weights matters a lot more than we expected when training data is limited. The full MoC system peaks at 73.8\% validation accuracy with a reasonably balanced routing distribution ($E_1$: 30.5\%, $E_2$: 20.5\%, $E_3$: 28.4\%, $E_4$: 20.6\%), but training too long causes it to drop to 62.2\%, so early stopping turns out to be essential. The QCGP temperature parameter $\tau_g$ barely moves during training ($\Delta\tau_g = -0.0011$), which explains why the gate attention maps look nearly uniform. Looking at 100 error cases, we find 48\% are perception errors and 41\% are reasoning errors, quite different from the 67\% perception-dominated profile that PAPO~\citep{wang2026papo} reports for RLVR-trained models.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

When a student looks at a circuit diagram and is asked ``which component controls the current?'', they know to focus on specific parts of the image. Modern vision-language models do not do this, their visual connector processes every image patch identically, no matter what the question is. This seems like a fundamental design flaw, and the numbers bear it out: around 67\% of multimodal reasoning failures come from getting the visual part wrong, not the reasoning~\citep{wang2026papo}. The language model ends up compensating for a noisy visual signal it was never designed to handle.

Existing connectors make different trade-offs but none of them condition on the question. LLaVA-1.5~\citep{liu2024llava} keeps all 576 image patches but ignores the question entirely. InstructBLIP~\citep{dai2023instructblip} does inject the question into its Q-Former, but the output is squeezed down to just 32 tokens — fine-grained spatial detail is lost in the process. MoE-based approaches~\citep{lin2024moellava, li2024cumo, pang2026mocha} add routing but route between experts that all have the same architecture, which limits what they can learn to specialize. As \citet{cha2024honeybee} point out, there is a fundamental tension between preserving spatial detail and compressing the token sequence  and the right choice probably depends on the question.

Our work tries to make the connector question-aware while keeping different architectural options available. We propose two things. First, the \emph{Question Conditioned Gating Projector} (QCGP), which uses the question to compute how relevant each image patch is, and gates the patch features accordingly  all without any compression. Second, the \emph{Mixture of Connectors} (MoC), a routing system that picks one of four very different connectors for each input: a frozen pretrained MLP, a Q-Former, a global summary token, or QCGP. The router is a lightweight 2-layer MLP that takes the question as input, and we use the load-balancing loss from \citet{fedus2022switch} to make sure all four experts actually get used.

What we found was both encouraging and humbling. On the positive side, the full MoC reached 73.8\% validation accuracy well above any single expert  and the router kept all four experts in use without collapse. On the less expected side, $E_2$, $E_3$, and $E_4$ performed poorly on their own (34--39\%) because they start from scratch with only 6{,}218 training samples, while $E_1$ benefits from millions of examples in LLaVA's pretraining. The QCGP gating also stayed near-uniform throughout training, suggesting the dataset was too small for the model to learn truly selective patch attention. These are useful findings that point to clear directions for future work.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:relwork}

The connector $\phi\colon \mathbb{R}^{N \times d_v} \to \mathbb{R}^{L \times d}$, mapping $N$ frozen-encoder patch tokens into $L$ LLM-space embeddings, is the axis this work modifies. Three lines of research contextualize our approach: the evolution of connector architectures, the conditioning of visual features on the input query, and the application of Mixture of Experts (MoE) to Vision-Language Models (VLMs).

\paragraph{Foundational Connector Designs.}
\citet{radford2021clip} established the dual-encoder paradigm; \citet{liang2022modgap} show that its contrastive objective creates a \emph{modality gap} that any downstream connector must bridge. \citet{liu2024llava} demonstrate that a two-layer MLP with GELU nonlinearity, applied identically to each patch token, achieves state-of-the-art performance on ScienceQA. The alternative philosophy of learned-query compression, originating with \citet{alayrac2022flamingo}'s Perceiver Resampler and formalized by \citet{li2023blip2}'s Q-Former, reduces the visual-token budget but irreversibly discards spatial structure. \citet{cha2024honeybee} formalize this locality-versus-compression trade-off and motivate a per-sample architecture selector.

\paragraph{Query-Conditioned Visual Feature Extraction.} All connectors discussed above are invariant to the input query $x^T$. \citet{ilievski2016fda} showed that question-driven region selection improves grounded VQA. \citet{dai2023instructblip} (InstructBLIP) scale this by injecting $x^T$ into Q-Former self-attention, achieving 90.7\% on ScienceQA-IMG, but this instruction-awareness is bottlenecked through $K$ compressed slots, irreversibly discarding fine-grained spatial structure. QCGP (our fourth expert $E_4$) instead conditions on the question at full patch resolution: an attention-pooled question vector $\mathbf{q}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^d$ produces per-patch cosine-similarity gates via learned $W_q, W_k$ bridging matrices, applying element-wise modulation while preserving full spatial resolution $L\!=\!N$ (Section~\ref{sec:method}). \citet{zhang2025sail} and \citet{zou2026vlsa} confirm that standard Vision-Language Pre-training does not close the cross-modal alignment gap, further motivating $E_4$'s bridging mechanism.

\paragraph{Mixture of Experts in VLMs.} \citet{shazeer2017moe} introduced sparsely-gated MoE; \citet{fedus2022switch} simplified it to top-1 routing with load-balancing loss. Recent MoE adaptations to VLMs take various structural approaches. MoE-LLaVA \citep{lin2024moellava} places MoE inside the LLM's FFN layers, leaving the connector untouched. CuMo \citep{li2024cumo} and MoCHA \citep{pang2026mocha} introduce routing at the connector level, but restrict their experts to identical architectural designs. MoVA \citep{zong2024mova} routes between multiple vision encoders, requiring several large frozen backbones. In contrast, our MoC uses a single shared CLIP encoder and routes inputs across four \emph{structurally distinct} connectors ($E_1$--$E_4$).

\paragraph{Hallucination, Perceptual Failures, and the ScienceQA Benchmark.} \citet{wang2026papo} (PAPO) find that approximately 67\% of errors in RLVR-trained MLLMs stem from visual misperception rather than flawed reasoning, consistent with the hallucination inequality $P_\theta(\text{ans} \mid x^T) \gg P_\theta(\text{ans} \mid x^V)$: the language prior overwrites visual evidence when the connector fails to ground query-relevant content. \citet{li2023pope} formalize this with the POPE diagnostic protocol to systematically quantify object hallucination. Our primary benchmark, ScienceQA \citep{lu2022scienceqa}, provides an image-required vs.\ text-only split that supports our visual grounding gap metric $\Delta_v$ and three-bucket failure analysis \citep{zhang2023multimodalcot}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Model Architecture and Visual Grounding Gap}

Figure~\ref{fig:arch} shows the full MoC pipeline. The system takes an image and a multiple-choice question as inputs. The image is processed by a frozen CLIP ViT-L/14 encoder (336$\times$336 resolution), which divides it into a 24$\times$24 grid and produces $N=576$ patch tokens $Z^V\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{N\times d_v}$ ($d_v=1024$). The question is tokenized by a frozen embedding layer, giving question tokens $U\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{T\times d}$. These question tokens are passed through a learned attention-pooling step to produce a compact question vector $\mathbf{q}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^d$, which drives both the router and the QCGP expert ($E_4$). The router, a small question-conditioned two-layer MLP reads $\mathbf{q}$ and selects one of four connector experts. The selected expert processes $Z^V$ (and $\mathbf{q}$ in the case of $E_4$) and produces visual tokens $\phi_k(Z^V)$ that are concatenated with the question tokens $U$ before being passed into the LLaVA-1.5-7B language model (Vicuna-1.5-7B, fine-tuned with LoRA). The model then generates the predicted answer as a single option letter.

To measure how much a model is actually using the image rather than guessing from the question text alone, we define the \emph{visual grounding gap}:
\begin{equation*}
    \Delta_v = \text{Acc}_{\text{image}} - \text{Acc}_{\text{text-only}},
\end{equation*}
where $\text{Acc}_{\text{text-only}}$ is accuracy when the image token is removed entirely. A higher $\Delta_v$ means the model is genuinely relying on visual evidence rather than language shortcuts.

\subsection{QLoRA Fine-Tuning Setup}

Since the full 7B model would not fit in GPU memory for training, we use QLoRA: the LLM backbone is quantized to 4-bit NF4 precision using \texttt{bitsandbytes}, and we only add small trainable LoRA adapters on the attention layers (\texttt{q\_proj}, \texttt{k\_proj}, \texttt{v\_proj}, \texttt{o\_proj}). The vision encoder, the original MLP projector, and the output head stay frozen throughout. We call \texttt{prepare\_model\_for\_kbit\_training} before \texttt{get\_peft\_model} this ordering matters for gradient checkpointing to work properly with quantized weights. The new connector modules ($E_2$, $E_3$, $E_4$), the router, and the question pooler are kept in float32 to avoid numerical overflow during softmax computations, while $E_1$ inherits the frozen float16 LLaVA projector. \subsection{Mixture of Connectors (MoC)}

The core idea of MoC is to replace the single fixed MLP projector with a pool of four experts and a router that picks one per input. Before routing, we extract a compact question vector $\mathbf{q}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^d$ from the LLM token embeddings $U\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{T\times d}$ using a learned attention-pooling query $\mathbf{q}_\mathrm{pool}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^d$:
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{q} = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{q}_\mathrm{pool}^\top U^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right)U \;\in \mathbb{R}^{d}.
\end{equation*}
This single vector summarizes what the question is asking and is shared between the router and $E_4$.

The four experts each take the CLIP patch tokens $Z^V\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{N\times d_v}$ and produce visual tokens in LLM space, but with very different designs:

\textbf{$E_1$ (MLP baseline, $L_1=576$):} This is simply LLaVA's original pretrained projector, kept frozen. $\phi_1(Z^V)_i = W_2\,\sigma(W_1 z_i^V)$. It adds no new trainable parameters and serves as the performance ceiling we are trying to match or beat.

\textbf{$E_2$ (Q-Former, $L_2=32$):} Inspired by BLIP-2~\citep{li2023blip2}, this expert uses $K=32$ learnable query vectors to attend over the 576 patches and compress them:
$\phi_2(Z^V) = \mathrm{softmax}(Q K_v^\top/\sqrt{d})\,V_v$, where $K_v=Z^VW_K$, $V_v=Z^VW_V$, $W_K,W_V\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{d_v\times d}$. It has 8{,}519{,}680 trainable parameters. The compression helps efficiency but loses spatial detail permanently.

\textbf{$E_3$ (global token, $L_3=1$):} The most aggressive compression, the entire image is summarized into a single token via attention pooling:
$\phi_3(Z^V) = W_{E_3}\sum_i \mathrm{softmax}_i(\mathbf{w}^\top z_i^V/\sqrt{d_v})\,z_i^V$. With 4{,}199{,}424 trainable parameters, this is the cheapest expert computationally, trading almost all spatial information for speed.

\textbf{$E_4$ (QCGP, $L_4=576$):} Our main contribution. The idea is to compute, for each of the 576 patches, how relevant it is to the current question, and then use that relevance to gate the patch features before projecting them. To do this properly, the question vector $\mathbf{q}$ (which lives in LLM space) and each patch token (which lives in CLIP space) need to be brought into the same coordinate system first. We project both into a shared $d_k=256$-dimensional subspace and $L_2$-normalize them to address the modality gap~\citep{liang2022modgap}:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_i = \mathrm{softmax}_i\!\left(\frac{\hat{u}^\top \hat{k}_i}{\tau_g}\right), \quad
  \mathbf{g}_i = \sigma\!\left(\alpha_i W_g + \mathbf{b}_g\right), \quad
  \phi_4(Z^V)_i = W_{E_4}\!\left(z_i^V \odot \mathbf{g}_i\right),
  \label{eq:qcgp}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{u}=W_q\mathbf{q}/\|W_q\mathbf{q}\|_2$, $\hat{k}_i=W_kz_i^V/\|W_kz_i^V\|_2$, $\tau_g$ is a learnable temperature scalar (initialized to 1.0), $W_g\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{d_v}$ controls per-channel gating strength, and $\sigma$ is Sigmoid. The gate $\mathbf{g}_i$ suppresses channels of patches that are irrelevant to the question. Unlike $E_2$ and $E_3$, QCGP outputs all 576 tokens, so no spatial detail is discarded. It has approximately 5{,}511{,}169 trainable parameters.

The \textbf{router} is a small two-layer MLP that reads the question vector $\mathbf{q}$ and outputs a probability over the four experts:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{r} = \mathrm{softmax}\!\left(W_r^{(2)}\,\mathrm{GELU}\!\left(W_r^{(1)}\mathbf{q}\right)\right) \in \mathbb{R}^4, \quad k^* = \arg\max_k r_k,
  \label{eq:router}
\end{equation}
where $W_r^{(1)}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{d_r\times d}$, $W_r^{(2)}\!\in\!\mathbb{R}^{4\times d_r}$, $d_r=64$. We initialize $W_r^{(2)}$ to all zeros so that routing starts uniformly at $r_k=0.25$ — this prevents one expert from dominating early training. Since $\arg\max$ has no gradient, we use a straight-through estimator to still allow the router weights to be updated.

One well-known problem with sparse routing is that the router can collapse to always picking the same expert, causing the others to never get trained. To prevent this, we add the load-balancing loss from \citet{fedus2022switch}:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{lb} = K\sum_{k=1}^{K} f_k \cdot p_k, \qquad \mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_\mathrm{CE} + \lambda_\mathrm{lb}\,\mathcal{L}_\mathrm{lb},
  \label{eq:lb}
\end{equation}
where $f_k$ is the fraction of the batch sent to expert $k$ (computed from discrete argmax, no gradient), and $p_k$ is the average router softmax probability for expert $k$ over the batch (has gradient). Multiplying the two means that if the router keeps sending everything to one expert, $p_k$ for that expert grows large, which pushes the loss up and forces the router to redistribute.

\section{Experimental Design}

\subsection{Research Questions}

We set out to answer three main questions. Did moving from 2{,}000-sample LoRA fine-tuning to the full 6{,}218-sample QLoRA setup improve results? Can connectors trained from random initialization compete with the pretrained MLP projector? And does combining all four experts under a learned router actually outperform any single expert on its own?

\subsection{Dataset and Evaluation}

We use ScienceQA (\texttt{derek-thomas/ScienceQA} on HuggingFace), keeping only the image-based questions. This gives us 6{,}218 training samples, 2{,}097 for validation, and 2{,}017 for testing. During development we evaluate on a 500-sample subset for speed; all final numbers use the full 2{,}017-sample test split. To extract the model's predicted answer, we use a cascade of regex patterns covering different response formats (e.g., ``(A)'', ``the answer is B'', standalone capital letters). The midterm baseline experiments were run locally, while all QLoRA training runs ($E_1$ through $E_4$ and the full MoC) were done on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU via Google Colab.

\section{Results and Findings}

\subsection{Single-Expert Ablations and Full MoC Results}

Table~\ref{tab:full} shows everything together, with the midterm results included at the top for context. Starting with the baselines: zero-shot LLaVA-1.5-7B gets 63.5\% image accuracy with a visual grounding gap of $\Delta_v=7.5\%$. LoRA fine-tuning on 2{,}000 samples kept accuracy roughly the same (62.6\%) but dropped text-only accuracy from 56.0\% to 51.0\%, which widened $\Delta_v$ to 11.6\%. In other words, fine-tuning made the model lean more on the image, even if the overall numbers barely moved.

Moving to the QLoRA results on the full dataset, $E_1$ reaches 65.6\% , the best single-expert number. The results for $E_2$, $E_3$, and $E_4$ are much lower: 34.0\%, 39.0\%, and 35.5\% respectively. This was the most striking finding of our experiments. The gap is not because these experts have worse architectures in principle, but because they start from random weights. $E_1$ inherits LLaVA's pretrained projector, which was trained on millions of image-text pairs. $E_2$, $E_3$, and $E_4$ only get 6{,}218 ScienceQA samples to learn from scratch. Interestingly, all four experts converge to almost the same training loss ($\approx$1.90--1.93 at step 750), which tells us the optimization is working fine, the problem is initialization, not training.

$E_4$ (QCGP) shows an interesting pattern: its image accuracy is low (35.5\%) but its text-only accuracy is the lowest of all (13.0\%), giving it the second-highest $\Delta_v$ of 22.5\%. This suggests the gating is doing something, it is more aggressively suppressing patches that the language model could have used as shortcuts,but it has not yet learned which patches actually contain the right visual information.

The full MoC hits 73.8\% validation accuracy at step 750, which is a significant jump over any single expert. But then something interesting happens: continuing to train past epoch 2 causes performance to drop. By step 1{,}900, image accuracy falls to 62.2\% and text accuracy actually exceeds it at 70.2\% ($\Delta_v=-8.0\%$). This means the model gradually shifted back to relying on language patterns as training went on too long. The best-checkpoint result shows the architecture genuinely works; the challenge is knowing when to stop.

Figure~\ref{fig:router} shows how often each expert was selected throughout training (cumulative counts over 1{,}900 steps and 30{,}440 routing decisions). The final breakdown is $E_1$: 30.5\%, $E_2$: 20.5\%, $E_3$: 28.4\%, $E_4$: 20.6\%. The training-time cumulative counts are the closest available measure of the router's learned behavior.

\subsection{$\tau_g$ Convergence}
\label{sec:taug}

Figure~\ref{fig:taug} tracks the learnable temperature $\tau_g$ across the 1{,}900 training steps. It started at 0.9996 and ended at 0.9985 — a total change of just 0.0011 — and stabilized well before training ended. To put this in perspective: a smaller $\tau_g$ would make the softmax sharper and focus attention on fewer patches; a larger one spreads attention more uniformly. The near-zero movement means the model chose to keep gating almost exactly as diffuse as it was initialized. This explains why the heatmaps in Figure~\ref{fig:heatmaps} look so flat. With only 6{,}218 training samples, the cross-entropy loss apparently does not generate enough signal to push $\tau_g$ away from its starting point.

\subsection{Three-Bucket Failure Analysis}
\label{sec:failure}

To understand where the model fails, we took $E_1$ (the best model on the final test set) and randomly sampled 100 errors from its 695 total mistakes on the full 2{,}017-sample test split (seed 42 for reproducibility). Each error was classified into one of three buckets: perception error (the model misread or ignored the image), reasoning error (the model saw the image correctly but drew the wrong conclusion), or CoT rescue (the error disappeared when we re-prompted with ``Let's think step by step.''). Figure~\ref{fig:failure} shows the breakdown: 48\% perception, 41\% reasoning, 11\% CoT rescue.

Compared to the PAPO~\citep{wang2026papo} finding of roughly 67\% perception errors in RLVR-trained models, our distribution is more balanced. ScienceQA requires applying actual science knowledge, not just reading diagrams correctly — so it makes sense that reasoning failures are more common here than in purely visual tasks. The 11\% CoT rescue rate is also interesting: for those cases, the model had the right information but needed a nudge to use it properly. Better prompting, not better visual grounding, would fix those.

\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why E1 won so easily.}
The fact that $E_1$ outperforms $E_2$, $E_3$, and $E_4$ by such a large margin (65.6\% vs.\ 34--39\%) was the most unexpected result of our experiments. All four experts converge to similar training loss, so $E_2$/$E_3$/$E_4$ are not failing to optimize — they are just starting from a much worse place. $E_1$ inherits a projector that was already trained on millions of image-text pairs during LLaVA's original training, meaning it already knows how to translate CLIP features into something the language model can understand. The new experts have to learn this from 6{,}218 samples, which is simply not enough. The practical lesson here is that for new connector architectures to compete in data-scarce settings, they need to be warm-started from the pretrained projector and only their unique components (the Q-Former queries, the global pooling weights, the QCGP gating layers) should be learned from scratch.

\paragraph{The MoC overfitting problem.}
Getting 73.8\% validation accuracy at epoch 2 but falling to 62.2\% by epoch 5 is a clear overfitting story. What makes it interesting is that the final $\Delta_v$ turns negative ($-8.0\%$): the model ends up relying \emph{more} on language than on images after too much training. Our interpretation is that the connector weights and LoRA adapters start to diverge after epoch 2 — the language model keeps adapting, but the connectors have already overfit to the training distribution, so their outputs become less and less useful to the LLM. The MoC architecture clearly has the capacity to do well (73.8\% shows that), but any future work needs to monitor validation accuracy and stop early.

\paragraph{QCGP did not learn to be selective.}
The $\tau_g$ result is the most honest finding to report: the QCGP temperature barely changed from 1.0, and the gate heatmaps are nearly flat. The gating mechanism is working in the sense that $E_4$ does suppress text-only accuracy more aggressively than other experts (13.0\% text accuracy vs.\ 33--60\% for others). But it has not learned to focus on the spatially correct regions. We think two things are happening: first, 6{,}218 samples may not be enough training signal; second, $W_q$ and $W_k$ start from random initialization, so early in training the cosine similarities $\hat{u}^\top\hat{k}_i$ are essentially random, and keeping $\tau_g\approx1.0$ is a safe response to avoid amplifying meaningless signals. If the projection weights were initialized from $E_1$'s pretrained weights, the cosine similarities would be more meaningful from the start, and $\tau_g$ might actually learn to adjust.

\paragraph{Perception vs. reasoning on ScienceQA.}
Our 48\% / 41\% / 11\% failure split is notably different from PAPO's 67\% perception finding. The difference makes sense: ScienceQA tests whether students understand scientific concepts, so even a model that reads a diagram correctly can fail if it does not know the underlying science. The MoC connector intervention directly addresses perception errors; the 41\% reasoning failures would require something beyond the visual representation, like chain-of-thought supervision or a dedicated reasoning module.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

The most significant constraint is the size of the training set relative to the number of new parameters we introduce. $E_2$, $E_3$, and $E_4$ together add 4--8.5 million trainable parameters each, yet they are trained on only 6{,}218 samples. This mismatch is the root cause of their underperformance and the near-neutral QCGP gating. Training on a larger multimodal dataset before fine-tuning on ScienceQA would likely change these results substantially. Additionally, all QLoRA experiments were run on a single A100 (Google Colab), which limits how much we could tune hyperparameters such as batch size, learning rate, and number of epochs were all constrained by session time limits and available memory. Finally, the router is trained jointly with the experts from the beginning, with no prior knowledge of which questions each expert handles best. This cold-start problem means early routing decisions are noisy, which adds noise to the load-balancing signal at the most critical phase of training.

\section{Conclusion}

This paper presented MoC, a system for routing visual inputs through four structurally different connector experts depending on the question being asked. The key results are worth restating plainly. $E_1$ wins by a large margin (65.6\%) because it is the only expert that benefits from LLaVA's pretrained projector weights — not because the MLP architecture is inherently better. The full MoC reaches 73.8\% validation accuracy and keeps all four experts active, demonstrating that question-conditioned routing across heterogeneous connectors is a viable and promising idea. However, training too long hurts, the final model at epoch 5 scores only 62.2\%, and early stopping at epoch 2 is essential. The QCGP temperature $\tau_g$ converges near its initialization, indicating the gating did not learn spatial selectivity on this dataset size. And the failure analysis shows that reasoning errors (41\%) are almost as common as perception errors (48\%) on ScienceQA, which means better connectors alone will not solve everything.

Going forward, the most important change would be to initialize $E_2$, $E_3$, and $E_4$ from $E_1$'s pretrained weights and only fine-tune their distinctive components. Larger training data and validation-based early stopping would also help the MoC realize its full potential.
